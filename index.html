Introduction

Accessibility means designing products, services, and technology so that they can be used by as many people as possible, regardless of their abilities or disabilities. In the context of user interface design, accessibility means designing interfaces that are easy to use and understand, regardless of a user's physical, sensory, or cognitive abilities.

Why Design for Accessibility?

According to the World Health Organization, there are over one billion people with disabilities globally, representing around 15% of the population. By designing with accessibility in mind, we can create products that are usable by a wider range of people, increasing engagement and satisfaction with our product or service.

What is Accessibility?

Accessibility is the practice of designing products and services that are usable by as many people as possible, regardless of their abilities or disabilities. In the context of user interfaces, accessibility means designing interfaces that can be used by people with different types of disabilities, such as visual, auditory, motor, or cognitive impairments.

Understanding User Needs

To design for accessibility, we need to understand the needs of users with disabilities. This involves identifying the barriers that prevent these users from using and accessing our product or service. We can use various research methods, such as user interviews, surveys, and usability testing, to gain insights into the needs and preferences of these users.

Guidelines for Designing

  1. Use clear and simple language
  2. Provide alternative text for images
  3. Use color contrast
  4. Provide keyboard shortcut
  5. Use accessible form controls
  6. Consider users with hearing impairments
  7. Test your design with users

Use Clear and Simple Language

When designing user interfaces, it's important to use clear and simple language that is easy to understand. This is particularly important for users with cognitive or language disabilities, who may find complex language or technical jargon difficult to understand.

Provide Alternative Text for Images

Alternative text is a text description that is provided for images, which can be read by screen readers or other assistive technologies. By providing alternative text, we can ensure that users with visual impairments can still understand the content of our product or service.

Use Color Contrast

Color contrast is the difference in color between the background and foreground elements of a design. By using sufficient color contrast, we can ensure that users with visual impairments can distinguish between different elements on the screen.

Provide Keyboard Shortcuts

Keyboard shortcuts are a useful way to provide alternative navigation options for users who may have difficulty using a mouse or trackpad. By providing keyboard shortcuts, we can ensure that all users can access all of the functionality of our product or service.

Use Accessible Form Controls

When designing forms, it's important to use accessible form controls, such as clear labels and error messages. By using accessible form controls, we can ensure that users with different types of disabilities can still use and interact with our product or service.

Consider Users with Hearing Impairments

Users with hearing impairments may find it difficult to access audio or video content. By providing captions or transcripts for this content, we can ensure that these users can still access the content and understand the information being presented.

Federal Regulations on Accessibility

The Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) of 1990 prohibits discrimination against people with disabilities in all areas of public life, including employment, transportation, and public accommodations.

In 2010, the Department of Justice published revised regulations under Title II and III of the ADA, which include requirements for state and local government websites and privately-owned websites that are considered places of public accommodation to be accessible to people with disabilities.

What is WCAG

WCAG stands for Web Content Accessibility Guidelines. It is a set of guidelines developed by the World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) that aim to make web content more accessible to people with disabilities. The guidelines provide recommendations for making websites, web applications, and other digital content more accessible to people with disabilities such as visual, auditory, physical, speech, cognitive, and neurological disabilities.

There are three levels of conformance defined in WCAG: A, AA, and AAA. Level A is the minimum level of accessibility that all websites should strive to meet, while Level AA and AAA provide additional recommendations for making content more accessible to people with disabilities.

What are ARIA Tags

ARIA stands for Accessible Rich Internet Applications. ARIA is a set of attributes that can be added to HTML elements to make web content more accessible to people with disabilities, particularly those who use assistive technologies such as screen readers, speech recognition software, and alternative input devices.

Some common ARIA attributes include:

  • aria-label: Specifies a text label for an element that is not visible on the screen, such as an icon or image.
  • aria-describedby: Associates an element with a description that provides additional information about its purpose or function.
  • aria-hidden: Specifies that an element should be hidden from assistive technologies, such as when a decorative image is used.

  • aria-expanded: Indicates whether a collapsible element, such as an accordion panel, is currently expanded or collapsed.
  • aria-disabled: Indicates whether an element is currently disabled and cannot be interacted with.

Accessibility Tools

Accessibility tools are software programs, plugins, or features that help users with disabilities to access digital content, including websites, mobile apps, and other types of software. These tools can help people with visual, auditory, physical, and cognitive disabilities to navigate and use digital content more effectively. Here are some examples of accessibility tools:

Screen readers - Screen readers are software programs that convert digital text into synthesized speech or braille output for users who are blind or have low vision. Popular screen readers include NVDA, JAWS, and VoiceOver.

Magnification software - Magnification software is designed to enlarge on-screen content for users with low vision. Popular magnification software includes ZoomText and MAGic.

Voice recognition software - Voice recognition software allows users to control their computer or mobile device using spoken commands. Popular voice recognition software includes Dragon NaturallySpeaking and Siri.

Captioning and transcription software - Captioning and transcription software automatically generates captions or transcripts for audio or video content, making it accessible to users who are deaf or hard of hearing. Popular captioning and transcription software includes Amara and Descript.

Color contrast checkers - Color contrast checkers are tools that can help web developers and designers ensure that the color contrast of their content meets accessibility guidelines. Popular color contrast checkers include WebAIM's Color Contrast Checker and Contrast Checker by Toptal.

Keyboard navigation tools - Keyboard navigation tools can help users with motor disabilities navigate digital content without using a mouse. Popular keyboard navigation tools include MouseKeys and Sticky Keys.
